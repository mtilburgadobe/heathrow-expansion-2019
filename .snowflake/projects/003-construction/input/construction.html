Future Operations

Running a three-runway Heathrow

Our proposals for runway alternation, night flight restrictions, directional preference and early growth set out how we will operate a larger Heathrow responsibly.

Share your views
Section 5.1

Runway alternation

During westerly operations we run a two-week alternation cycle, switching runways at 3pm each day. Every week communities under both runway paths get predictable respite from aircraft noise for half the day.

When the wind blows from the west (about 70% of the year), planes land and take off towards the west. Our current two-runway alternation switches the landing runway and take-off runway at 3pm, so communities get respite in either the morning or afternoon on alternate weeks.

At the end of each week we switch completely — what we did in the evening the previous week, we now do in the morning, and vice versa. This gives each community a full week of morning respite and a full week of evening respite.

With three runways, we will extend this further. The proposed pattern runs in a four-day cycle through four mode allocations, each defining whether each runway is used for arrivals, departures, or mixed-mode operations. This rotation ensures communities at the ends of all three runways receive regular breaks from overflying regardless of wind direction.

We are also proposing Reflective Alternation: when the wind changes direction mid-cycle, communities continue to receive the respite they were expecting — something not possible today because of the legacy Cranford Agreement.

What is the Cranford Agreement?

An agreement between the Government and residents of Cranford which restricted northerly runway take-offs in an easterly direction over the village. It is no longer in place. With a third runway we will be able to alternate runways even during easterly operations, delivering respite for communities that have not had it before.

What is mode allocation?

Mode allocation describes how runway modes (arrivals-only, departures-only, or mixed) are distributed across all three runways at any one time. With three runways we can rotate through four different mode allocations, giving communities at the end of each runway a predictable break from aircraft overhead.

Section 5.2

Ban on scheduled night flights

6.5hr
ban on scheduled night flights from 23:00 to 05:30 — the longest scheduled ban at any hub airport in Europe, using a single runway for early morning arrivals to give each community at least 7 hours' respite between 22:00 and 07:00.

Night flights are an important part of operations at airports worldwide. Time-zone differences mean it is difficult to avoid arrivals and departures at night and early in the morning. However, the Airports NPS sets out the Government's expectation that with expansion we should have a 6.5-hour ban on scheduled flights.

Scheduled times are the times shown on your departure board. Runway time is when the aircraft actually touches down or lifts off — typically 10–20 minutes later due to taxiing.

Early morning arrivals from 05:30 will use a single runway only. Feedback from previous consultations strongly preferred the 05:30 start over 05:45 because fewer communities would be overflown. The runway used will change each morning in line with our alternation proposals, ensuring communities each get at least 7 hours' respite.

Recovery periods allow Heathrow to manage aircraft delayed into the night period, using one runway each for: departures from 23:00 until midnight (1 hour of recovery beyond the last scheduled departure), and arrivals from 23:00 until 23:30 (30 minutes of recovery beyond the last scheduled arrival). This reduces the risk of disruption to passengers and the airline network.

Section 5.3

Directional preference

The direction aircraft arrive and depart from depends on the wind — for safety, aircraft take off and land into the wind. Currently a 'westerly preference' applies when winds are light (below 5 knots), meaning aircraft continue to operate towards the west even when it would be technically safe to go in either direction.

On an expanded Heathrow, we propose to replace westerly preference with a 'managed preference'. This will be based on a set of criteria — still to be determined — designed to limit overall noise effects on communities and deliver longer periods of respite when combined with runway alternation.

The managed preference applies only to the 20% of time when winds are below 5 knots and aircraft can safely operate in either direction. It does not apply to the remaining 80% when wind direction is determinative.

Section 5.4

Early growth

The Airports NPS identifies the urgent need for increased airport capacity. Before the new runway opens we propose to bring some of the additional capacity forward on our existing two runways — up to 25,000 additional Air Transport Movements per year.

Heathrow is currently capped at 480,000 ATMs per year. We have been operating at around 98% of that cap since 2005, to the detriment of consumer and economic interests. The main part of expansion is the new third runway, which will not be open before 2026 — 11 years after the Airports Commission recommended it.

New navigation technology allows us to make more efficient use of our existing two runways. In September 2016 we published proposals for up to 25,000 additional ATMs on existing infrastructure, and we want to deliver those benefits as soon as practicable.

To ensure early growth does not adversely affect communities or the environment, we propose the following commitments:

  • Our Environmentally Managed Growth strategy will apply from the outset of early growth
  • Our Noise Insulation Scheme will be introduced from the beginning of early growth
  • Surface Access Proposals will be developed ensuring no more airport-related traffic on roads than today
  • The night flight ban from 05:30 will apply for three runways (not just two) from the start of early growth

Maintaining airport efficiency during early growth

We will continue to invest in development of infrastructure, technology and people; retain our current scheduling processes to mitigate delays; and balance additional flights in peak and off-peak periods with our operational performance and resilience commitments.

Section 6

Surface access proposals

Expansion is a unique opportunity to change how people and goods travel to, from and around the airport. We are committed to delivering expansion with no more airport-related traffic on roads than today, and at least 50% of passenger trips by public transport by 2030.

🚇

Public transport

Making best use of existing services including Crossrail (Elizabeth Line) and Heathrow Express, supporting committed improvements, and developing new routes including a Western Rail Link and Southern Rail Access to Heathrow.

🚲

Colleague travel

Fundamentally changing how over 76,000 on-site colleagues travel to work. New cycle lanes, expanded Heathrow Cycle Hub, and relocating employment to public-transport-accessible locations to reduce car trips by 25% by 2030 and 50% by 2040.

🚗

Car parking

Consolidating car parking at parkways around the airport with high-frequency links to terminals. Phased reduction of colleague parking per passenger, with increased provision for electric and zero-emission vehicles.

🏙️

Road user charging

A Heathrow Ultra Low Emissions Zone (HULEZ) by 2022, then a Heathrow Vehicle Access Charge from the opening of the third runway, applying to all passenger cars, taxis and private hire vehicles entering car parks and forecourts.

🚕

Taxi & private hire

Taxi and private hire will remain important for areas with limited public transport. We will increase 'backfilling' — matching passengers to drivers in both directions — to minimise empty vehicle miles and improve efficiency.

🚛

Freight & intelligent mobility

Doubling cargo capacity while minimising cargo vehicle trips through consolidation. Intelligent Mobility innovations including a Heathrow Travel Wallet and enhanced real-time transport information for colleagues and passengers.
